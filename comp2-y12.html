Milgram (1963)
Obedience to Authority — Behavioural Study of Obedience
📌 Key Details
ResearcherStanley Milgram
Year1963
AimTo investigate how far ordinary people would obey an authority figure even when required to inflict harm on an innocent person
Sample40 male participants, aged 20–50, recruited via newspaper advert, Yale University
MethodControlled observation / lab experiment
Procedure
  • Participants recruited via newspaper advert for a memory study at Yale University ($4.50 payment)
  • On arrival, participant met a confederate (Mr Wallace) — draw was rigged so participant always became the Teacher
  • Confederate (Learner) was strapped to chair in adjacent room with electrodes attached
  • Teacher administered a word-pair memory test via intercom
  • For each wrong answer, Teacher was instructed to give an electric shock, increasing in 15-volt increments from 15V to 450V
  • Shock generator had labels: Slight Shock → Danger: Severe Shock → XXX
  • Confederate gave scripted responses (grunts, protests, silence at 315V+)
  • Experimenter in a grey coat used four verbal prods if participant hesitated
📋 The Four Prods
  1. "Please continue" / "Please go on"
  2. "The experiment requires you to continue"
  3. "It is absolutely essential that you continue"
  4. "You have no other choice but to continue"
Results
  • 65% of participants delivered the maximum 450V shock
  • 100% continued to at least 300V
  • All participants showed signs of stress — sweating, trembling, nervous laughter
  • No participant stopped before 300V
  • Prior prediction by psychiatrists: only 1–2% would go to 450V
📊 Situational Variables (Variations)
ConditionObedience Rate
Original (Yale, voice feedback)65%
Proximity (same room as learner)40%
Touch proximity (hand on plate)30%
Remote authority (experimenter by phone)20.5%
Run-down office block (not Yale)47.5%
Ordinary man gives orders (no uniform)20%
Agency Theory

Milgram proposed that humans exist in one of two states:

  • Autonomous state — acting according to own values, taking personal responsibility
  • Agentic state — acting as an agent of an authority figure, passing responsibility upward

The shift from autonomous to agentic state is called an agentic shift. Once in the agentic state, people experience moral strain but continue to obey due to binding factors (e.g. politeness, prior commitment, obligation to science).

🔑 Legitimacy of Authority

People are more likely to obey when the authority figure is seen as legitimate — i.e. having the right to give orders (e.g. a doctor, police officer, or Yale University experimenter). Legitimacy is enhanced by uniform, title, and institutional setting.

🔗 Supporting Study: Hofling et al. (1966)

Nurses were telephoned by an unknown 'Dr Smith' and told to administer 20mg of Astroten (double the maximum dose on the bottle). 21/22 nurses obeyed without questioning. Supports real-world obedience to authority. When asked beforehand, nurses said they would NOT obey — this shows the power of situational factors.

✅ Strengths
  • High internal validity — standardised procedure, controlled environment; same script for all participants
  • Replication — the study has been replicated (Burger, 2009) with similar findings, supporting reliability
  • Real-world application — helps explain atrocities such as the Holocaust; Milgram argued ordinary people can commit extraordinary harm when in the agentic state
  • Supported by variations — systematic variations demonstrate clear situational determinants of obedience
❌ Weaknesses
  • Ethical issues — participants were deceived, not given the right to withdraw, and experienced significant psychological stress; BPS guidelines were violated
  • Demand characteristics — participants may have guessed the shocks were fake; some reported not believing the set-up
  • Androcentric sample — original study used only men; Milgram's female sample also showed 65% obedience, but the original findings cannot be generalised to women
  • Cultural bias — sample was American; cross-cultural replications show varying rates (e.g. higher obedience in Germany, lower in Australia)
  • Low ecological validity — lab setting is artificial; obedience in real workplaces or institutions may differ
Describe the procedure of Milgram's (1963) study. [4]
4 marksAO1
Milgram recruited 40 male participants via a newspaper advert, telling them it was a study of memory and learning at Yale University. On arrival, participants were told they would be the 'Teacher' while a confederate was the 'Learner'. The learner was strapped to a chair in an adjacent room with electrodes attached. The teacher read word pairs and had to administer electric shocks for incorrect answers, increasing in 15-volt increments from 15V to 450V. The experimenter used verbal prods to encourage continuation if participants hesitated. No real shocks were given — the learner was a confederate who gave scripted responses.
Explain what is meant by 'agentic state'. Use an example from Milgram's study. [3]
3 marksAO1AO2
The agentic state is when an individual sees themselves as an agent acting on behalf of a legitimate authority figure, rather than taking personal responsibility for their own actions. In Milgram's study, participants shifted into an agentic state when they were instructed by the experimenter (seen as a legitimate authority due to his white coat and Yale affiliation) to continue administering shocks. Despite showing signs of distress, 65% continued to 450V, suggesting they had passed moral responsibility to the experimenter.
Evaluate Milgram's (1963) study, with reference to ethical issues. [6]
6 marksAO3
One ethical issue with Milgram's study is deception — participants were told it was a memory study and were not informed the shocks were fake, or that the learner was a confederate. This violates the ethical principle of informed consent. Furthermore, participants were not given a genuine right to withdraw; the verbal prods ('You have no other choice but to continue') could be seen as coercive. Participants also experienced significant psychological distress — trembling, sweating, and nervous laughter were observed. However, Milgram argued that participants were fully debriefed afterwards and 84% reported being glad to have taken part. He also followed up with a psychiatrist who found no lasting harm. Nevertheless, by modern BPS standards, the study would not receive ethical approval, and Milgram was criticised by the APA at the time.
Kohlberg (1958/1963)
Stages of Moral Development
📌 Key Details
ResearcherLawrence Kohlberg
Year1958 (PhD thesis), extended 1963+
AimTo develop a stage theory of moral reasoning across childhood and adulthood
Sample72 boys aged 10–16 from Chicago, later followed longitudinally
MethodLongitudinal study using semi-structured interviews with moral dilemmas
Background

Kohlberg was influenced by Piaget's work on cognitive development. He argued that moral reasoning develops in invariant stages — everyone moves through the same sequence, though not everyone reaches the highest stages. It is the reasoning behind a decision that determines stage, not the decision itself.

Level 1: Pre-conventional Morality
Stage 1 — Punishment and Obedience

Rules are obeyed to avoid punishment. The child is egocentric and does not consider others' perspectives. "I won't steal because I'll get into trouble."

Stage 2 — Instrumental Purpose

Rules obeyed for personal reward/gain. Beginning to consider others' needs, but only in a self-serving way. "I'll help you if you help me."

Level 2: Conventional Morality
Stage 3 — Good Boy/Good Girl

Behaviour aimed at gaining approval from others. Being seen as a good person. Living up to what is expected. "I want people to think well of me."

Stage 4 — Law and Order

Rules and laws must be upheld for society to function. Authority and social order are paramount. "If everyone broke the rules, society would collapse."

Level 3: Post-conventional Morality
Stage 5 — Social Contract

Laws are seen as social agreements that can be changed democratically. Individual rights are important. "Rules exist to serve people, not the other way around."

Stage 6 — Universal Ethical Principles

Moral decisions based on abstract, universal principles (justice, equality, human dignity) — even if they conflict with law. Kohlberg saw this as rare. "I act according to my conscience, even if it means breaking the law."

The Heinz Dilemma

A woman was dying from a rare cancer. A druggist had discovered a drug that might save her, but was charging 10x the cost. Her husband, Heinz, could only raise half the money. The druggist refused to sell cheaper. Should Heinz steal the drug?

🎯 Important — It's the reasoning that matters, not the answer

Kohlberg was interested in HOW participants justified their response, not whether they said yes or no. The same answer (e.g. "Heinz should steal") can reflect different stages depending on the reasoning given.

Example Responses by Stage
StageExample reasoning
1"He shouldn't steal — he'll be arrested and punished"
2"He should steal — he needs his wife alive to look after him"
3"He should steal — a good husband would do anything to save his wife"
4"He shouldn't steal — stealing is against the law and everyone must obey rules"
5"He should steal — the right to life outweighs property rights in extreme cases"
6"He must steal — the universal principle of preserving human life demands it"
✅ Strengths
  • Longitudinal support — Kohlberg's own follow-up studies and Colby et al. (1983) tracking participants over 20 years found consistent stage progression, supporting the theory's validity
  • Cross-cultural support — Snarey (1985) reviewed studies across 27 cultures; pre-conventional and conventional stages appeared universal, supporting the idea that stages are biologically influenced
  • Widely applied — used in moral education, restorative justice programmes, and understanding criminal behaviour
❌ Weaknesses
  • Gender bias (Gilligan, 1982) — Kohlberg's original sample was all male. Carol Gilligan argued that women reason differently — using an ethic of care (relationships and responsibilities) rather than an ethic of justice. Women appeared to score lower on Kohlberg's scale but this reflects bias in the scoring system, not inferior morality
  • Cultural bias — Stage 6 (universal ethical principles) reflects Western individualist values; collectivist cultures may emphasise community harmony over abstract individual rights, yet score lower
  • Moral reasoning ≠ moral behaviour — knowing what is right does not mean people act on it; the theory does not predict real-world moral behaviour
  • Hypothetical dilemmas — using artificial dilemmas like the Heinz story may not reflect how people reason in real moral situations
  • Androcentric and ethnocentric — doubly biased in both gender and cultural terms
Outline two stages of Kohlberg's theory of moral development. [4]
4 marksAO1
Stage 1 (Punishment and Obedience): At this pre-conventional stage, individuals obey rules solely to avoid punishment. The child is egocentric and cannot consider others' perspectives; moral decisions are based on consequences for themselves. For example, "I won't steal because I will be punished." Stage 4 (Law and Order): At this conventional stage, individuals believe that laws and social rules must be obeyed for society to function. Morality is defined by following established authority and maintaining social order. For example, "Heinz should not steal because it is against the law and breaking rules leads to social breakdown."
Evaluate Kohlberg's theory of moral development. [8]
8 marksAO3
A strength of Kohlberg's theory is the longitudinal support it has received. Colby et al. (1983) followed Kohlberg's original participants for over 20 years and found that participants progressed through the stages in the predicted order without skipping stages, supporting the idea that moral development is invariant and universal. However, Gilligan (1982) criticised the theory for gender bias. Kohlberg's original sample consisted only of males, and Gilligan argued that his scoring system was biased towards a justice-based approach to morality that is more typical of men. She proposed that women reason using an 'ethic of care', prioritising relationships and responsibilities, which would score lower on Kohlberg's scale. This suggests the theory does not represent female moral development accurately. Additionally, Snarey (1985) reviewed studies across 27 cultures and found that pre-conventional and conventional stages were universal, offering cross-cultural support. However, Stage 6 (universal ethical principles) was rarely found outside Western, individualist societies, suggesting the higher stages may reflect cultural bias rather than a universal endpoint of moral development. A further weakness is that the theory measures moral reasoning rather than moral behaviour — an individual can reason at a high stage but still act immorally in real situations, limiting the theory's practical predictive power.

BPS Ethical Principles
  • Informed consent — participants must agree to take part knowing what is involved
  • Deception — must be avoided unless absolutely necessary; requires full debrief
  • Right to withdraw — participants can leave at any time without penalty
  • Protection from harm — psychological and physical well-being must be protected
  • Confidentiality — personal data must not be disclosed without consent
  • Debriefing — participants should be told the true nature of the study afterwards, especially if deception was used
  • Privacy — participants have a right not to be observed without consent in private settings
⚠️ Cost-Benefit Analysis

Sometimes a study has scientific value that justifies mild ethical costs — e.g. mild deception in Milgram produced important findings about obedience. Researchers must weigh the potential benefits against the ethical costs. The BPS guidelines are the standard in the UK.

Inferential Statistical Tests
🎯 Decision Tree

To choose the right test, ask:

  1. Is the study looking for a difference or a correlation/association?
  2. What level of data was collected? (Nominal / Ordinal / Interval)
  3. If a difference — what design was used? (Related/repeated measures or unrelated/independent groups)
TestUse when...
Chi-squared (χ²)Difference, nominal data, independent groups
Mann-Whitney UDifference, ordinal/interval data, independent groups
Wilcoxon Signed-RanksDifference, ordinal/interval data, repeated measures
Spearman's Rho (rₛ)Correlation, ordinal/interval data
Pearson's rCorrelation, interval data, normal distribution
Significance Testing
  • p ≤ 0.05 — the standard significance level in psychology (5% probability the result is due to chance)
  • p ≤ 0.01 — more stringent; used when greater confidence is needed
  • The calculated value is compared to a critical value from a statistical table
⚠️ Critical Value Rules — Know These!
  • Mann-Whitney / Wilcoxon / Chi-squared: calculated value must be equal to or less than (≤) the critical value to be significant
  • Spearman's Rho / Pearson's r: calculated value must be equal to or greater than (≥) the critical value to be significant
📋 How to Write a Conclusion

State: (1) whether the result is significant; (2) whether to accept or reject the null hypothesis; (3) reference the p value used.

Example: "The calculated value of U = 12 is less than the critical value of 19 at p ≤ 0.05 (one-tailed). Therefore, the result is significant and we can reject the null hypothesis. There is a significant difference between the two conditions."

Type 1 and Type 2 Errors
ErrorWhat happenedWhen more likely
Type 1 (False positive)Rejected the null hypothesis when it was actually trueSignificance level too lenient (e.g. p ≤ 0.10)
Type 2 (False negative)Accepted the null hypothesis when it was actually falseSignificance level too stringent (e.g. p ≤ 0.001)
A researcher conducts an experiment using a repeated measures design. Identify an appropriate statistical test and justify your choice. [3]
3 marksAO2
An appropriate test would be the Wilcoxon Signed-Ranks test. This is because the study uses a repeated measures design (related data), and if the data collected is at ordinal or interval level, Wilcoxon is the correct non-parametric test for looking for a difference between two related conditions. If the data were interval and normally distributed, a related t-test would be appropriate, but Wilcoxon is the standard choice for most psychology investigations.
Explain what is meant by a Type 1 error. [2]
2 marksAO1
A Type 1 error (false positive) occurs when a researcher rejects the null hypothesis when it is actually true — i.e. concluding there is a significant effect when in reality the result was due to chance. This is more likely when the significance level is set too leniently (e.g. p ≤ 0.10).

⚠️ What are Personal Investigations?

Each year Eduqas sets two investigation titles. You carry out both in class, then answer questions about your specific investigations in the exam. Every answer must reference the actual co-variables, IV/DV, sample, and methods from your investigations — not generic research methods.

Investigation 1: Content Analysis of 6 Nations Post-Match Interviews
TopicAnalysis of language used in post-match press conference interviews
MethodContent analysis
AimTo analyse the frequency/type of language used by winning vs losing coaches/players in 6 Nations post-match interviews
HypothesisThere will be a difference in the frequency of [e.g. positive/negative language / attribution language] used by winning teams compared to losing teams
Null hypothesisThere will be no significant difference in language use between winning and losing teams
Design Decisions
DecisionChoice & Justification
Type of content analysisFrequency count of pre-defined categories (e.g. positive attributions, negative attributions, external attributions)
Sampling of materialSystematic sampling of interviews — e.g. first 5 minutes of post-match press conferences, same matches, same tournament
Categories/coding frameOperational definitions of each category must be clear and unambiguous to ensure inter-rater reliability
Level of dataNominal (frequency counts in categories)
Statistical testChi-squared (χ²) — testing for difference, nominal data, independent groups (winning vs losing)
Significance levelp ≤ 0.05
One- or two-tailedOne-tailed if direction predicted in hypothesis; two-tailed if non-directional
📊 Graphs

Use a bar chart to display frequency counts across categories for winning vs losing teams. Bar charts are appropriate for nominal (categorical) data. Do NOT use a scatter graph or histogram.

Ethical Considerations
  • No direct ethical issues — content analysis uses publicly available material (TV broadcasts, press conference footage). Participants are not directly involved
  • Confidentiality — individuals should still be anonymised in write-up if focusing on specific responses
  • Respect — care should be taken not to misrepresent findings about named athletes or coaches
📝 Strengths of Content Analysis
  • Uses real-world data — high ecological validity as interviews are naturally occurring
  • Relatively easy to replicate (if coding frame is published)
  • Non-invasive — no ethical issues around consent or harm to participants
📝 Weaknesses / Improvements
  • Subjectivity — categorising language can be subjective; improve by using two independent coders and checking inter-rater reliability (e.g. Cohen's Kappa or % agreement)
  • Limited sample — only a few matches; increase ecological validity by including more teams/seasons
  • Cultural variation — coaching styles vary by nation; confound the comparison
  • Cause and effect cannot be established — content analysis cannot show that winning causes different language use
Exam Q Practice
Identify the co-variables in your content analysis investigation. [2]
The co-variables were (1) the outcome of the match (win or loss) and (2) the frequency/type of language used in post-match interviews. As this is a content analysis/correlation-like comparison rather than a true experiment, there is no IV and DV — instead we compare frequency counts across the two outcome groups.
Explain why a Chi-squared test was used to analyse the data from your content analysis. [3]
Chi-squared was used because the investigation looked for a difference in language use between winning and losing teams, the data was at the nominal level (frequency counts in discrete categories), and the two groups (winners and losers) were independent — different teams were in each group. Chi-squared is the appropriate non-parametric test for these conditions.
Investigation 2: Social Facilitation Experiment
TopicEffect of an audience on task performance (social facilitation)
MethodLab experiment (or controlled experiment in school)
BackgroundZajonc (1965) — the presence of others increases arousal; this improves performance on simple/well-learned tasks but impairs performance on complex/novel tasks (drive theory)
IVPresence or absence of an audience while completing a task
DVPerformance on the task (e.g. time to complete, number correct, accuracy score)
DesignIndependent groups OR repeated measures (discuss which was used)
Level of dataInterval (e.g. time in seconds) or ordinal (ranked scores)
Statistical Test Choice
If Independent Groups Design was used:

Mann-Whitney U — testing for difference, ordinal/interval data, unrelated (independent) samples

If Repeated Measures Design was used:

Wilcoxon Signed-Ranks — testing for difference, ordinal/interval data, related (repeated measures) samples

⚠️ Critical Value Direction

For both Mann-Whitney and Wilcoxon: the calculated value must be equal to or LESS THAN (≤) the critical value to be significant.

📊 Graphs

Use a bar chart to show mean (or median) performance scores for audience vs no-audience conditions. If data is interval and normally distributed, a mean and SD table can also be shown. Do NOT use a scatter graph (that is for correlations).

Controls (Extraneous Variables)
  • Same task — identical task used in both conditions to prevent task difficulty being a confound
  • Time of day — test both conditions at the same time to control for arousal/fatigue differences
  • Audience size and behaviour — standardise number of observers and their behaviour (e.g. silent observation)
  • Practice effects (repeated measures) — counterbalancing: half do audience first, half do no-audience first
  • Noise / distraction — same room conditions for both groups
📝 Strengths
  • High control over extraneous variables in a lab/school setting — increases internal validity
  • Standardised procedure allows replication
  • Can establish cause and effect — IV clearly manipulated
📝 Weaknesses / Improvements
  • Demand characteristics — participants may guess the aim and alter behaviour; use a single-blind procedure
  • Low ecological validity — artificial task may not reflect real-world performance under observation
  • Small sample — class-based study limits generalisability; increase N or use random sampling
  • Individual differences — if independent groups, differences between participants could confound results; matched pairs or repeated measures design would control this
Ethical Considerations
  • Informed consent — participants (and guardians if under 16) must consent
  • Deception — if the true aim is withheld to avoid demand characteristics, a full debrief must be given afterwards
  • Right to withdraw — participants must be reminded they can stop at any time
  • Protection from harm — performance anxiety must be considered; reassure participants there are no right/wrong answers
Exam Q Practice
Operationalise the IV and DV in your social facilitation investigation. [2]
IV: the presence or absence of an audience — operationalised as either (a) completing the task alone in a room, or (b) completing the task with [X] observers seated silently watching. DV: task performance — operationalised as the time taken (in seconds) to complete [describe the task], or the number of correct responses within [time limit].
Explain one way you could improve your social facilitation investigation. [3]
One improvement would be to increase the sample size. The investigation used a small class-based sample which limits the generalisability of the findings. By using a larger, randomly selected sample drawn from the wider school population, the findings would be more representative and it would be possible to draw more reliable conclusions about whether the effect of an audience on performance applies to a broader population.
